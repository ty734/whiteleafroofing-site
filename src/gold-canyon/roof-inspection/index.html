The sun damage roof check

What I look for on an exposed roof

Gold Canyon roofs live in full sun on open desert. When I walk one, I'm checking five things: the tiles themselves, the condition of the underlayment, the flashing at chimneys and valleys, every penetration (vents, skylights, satellite mounts), and the fascia.

The underlayment is the one that matters most out here. On a tile roof it's the actual waterproofing, and this kind of exposure cooks it faster than the same felt lasts in shadier neighborhoods. From the street everything looks perfect. Up close, I can tell you whether you've got years left or whether it's time to plan for a tile underlayment replacement.

You get photos of what I find and a plain-English verdict: leave it alone, repair it, or start thinking about replacement. Whatever the roof actually needs, that's what I'll say.

When should a Gold Canyon roof get inspected?

Before monsoon season, after any storm that pushes wind across the foothills, before selling your home, and any time the roof is 15 or more years old. With this much sun exposure I'd rather find a worn spot in May than meet it as a ceiling stain in August.

What does sun damage look like on a tile roof?

From the driveway, usually like nothing. The tiles shrug off UV, but the underlayment beneath them cooks, and in full-sun exposure like Gold Canyon's it cooks faster. Up close I can spot brittle felt, cracked flashing seals, and tiles the wind has nudged out of place, and I'll show you photos of all of it.

What happens after the inspection?

You get photos and a plain answer: leave it alone, repair it, or start planning a replacement. If work is needed, most repairs run $500 to $3,500 and replacements get a free individual bid. If nothing is needed, I tell you that and go home.
