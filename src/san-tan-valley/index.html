Local knowledge

A community of first roofs

San Tan Valley grew up in two big waves, the 2000s and the 2010s, when neighborhoods like Johnson Ranch, San Tan Heights, Copper Basin, Pecan Creek and Skyline Ranch filled in the desert east of Queen Creek almost overnight. Nearly every roof out here is concrete tile, and nearly all of it went on over whatever felt underlayment the builder was buying by the truckload that year.

That felt is the whole story. The tiles will outlive your mortgage, but builder-grade underlayment ages out in Arizona heat on its own schedule, and the 2000s-wave homes are reaching it now. The honest fix is a tile underlayment replacement: lift your tiles, replace the felt, relay the same tiles. Decades more roof without paying for tile you already own.

The other thing about San Tan Valley: most of you commute. You leave before sunrise, come home after dark, and nobody's staring at the roof in between. Out here the first sign of a roof problem is usually a ceiling stain, not the storm that caused it, weeks earlier.

And with open desert running right up to the San Tan Mountains, monsoon wind and dust storms arrive with nothing to slow them down. After a big one, get the roof looked at. Mine is the free kind of look.

My San Tan Valley home was built in the 2000s. Why is the roof having problems already?

Most San Tan Valley roofs are concrete tile over builder-grade felt underlayment, and Arizona heat wears out that felt long before the tiles. The tile is fine. The waterproofing under it is what ages out, and the first wave of those roofs is hitting that point now. A free inspection will tell you how much time yours realistically has.
