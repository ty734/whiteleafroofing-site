SPF basics, in plain English

How a foam roof actually works

SPF stands for spray polyurethane foam. It goes on as a liquid, expands into a seamless, insulating layer over your whole flat roof, and then gets sealed with a protective elastomeric coating. No seams means no seam leaks, and the insulation value is a real advantage when it's 115 outside. I explain the whole system in my post on what a foam roof is.

Here's the part that matters: the foam is not what protects your house from the sun. The coating is. Arizona UV slowly wears that coating down, and once it's gone, the sun attacks the exposed foam directly. It yellows, gets brittle, pits, and starts absorbing water. At that point you're not maintaining a roof anymore, you're replacing one.

Flat roofs are everywhere in the East Valley for good reasons (little rain, no snow, southwestern style), and I cover that history in why Arizona houses have flat roofs. But flat means the roof depends entirely on its coating and drainage. That's the trade-off, and I lay the rest out honestly in foam roof disadvantages.

The honest part

Recoat vs. replace, told straight

Some companies look at a tired foam roof and quote a full tear-off, because that's the bigger ticket. My approach is different: if the foam underneath is still sound, a recoat is the right call and that's what I'll recommend. The coating cycle (commonly somewhere in the 5 to 7 year range depending on product and exposure) is exactly what keeps you from ever needing the expensive version.

The flip side is also true, and I'll say it to your face: if the foam has been bare to the sun for years and it's degraded or waterlogged, a recoat is just painting over a problem. You'd be paying twice. That's when replacement genuinely makes more sense, and I'll show you the evidence on your own roof, not a scare-tactic brochure.

Either way the inspection is free and I do it personally. Sun damage builds slowly here, so if you can't remember the last time your foam roof was coated, that's usually the answer. I'm based in Chandler and work flat roofs all over Gilbert and the rest of the East Valley.

Foam roof questions

What flat roof owners ask

What is a foam (SPF) roof?

Spray polyurethane foam. It's sprayed on as a liquid, expands into a seamless insulating layer, and gets sealed with a protective elastomeric coating. That coating, not the foam itself, is what takes the beating from Arizona sun. Full explainer: what is a foam roof.

How often does a foam roof need to be recoated?

The coating wears down under UV and needs reapplying on a regular cycle, commonly somewhere in the 5 to 7 year range depending on the product and sun exposure. I inspect the coating free and tell you honestly whether it's due, overdue, or fine for a while.

Should I recoat my foam roof or replace it?

If the foam underneath is sound, recoat. It restores protection for a fraction of the cost. If the foam has been exposed too long and is degraded or soaked across large areas, replacement makes more sense and I'll show you why on your own roof. The trade-offs are covered in foam roof disadvantages.

Why does my Arizona house have a flat roof?

Little rain, no snow load, and southwestern style. Flat roofs work well here, but they depend on their coating and drainage, so maintenance matters more than on a tile roof. The backstory: why Arizona houses have flat roofs.

Can you repair ponding water and cracks on a foam roof?

Yes. Cracks, blisters, bird pecks and low spots that pond water are all repairable when caught early. Small foam repairs plus a fresh coat cost far less than dealing with a saturated roof later. Most repairs run $500 to $3,500.

Yes. I inspect the roof personally and give you a straight recoat-or-replace answer. No cost, no obligation, no pressure.
