Metal roofing pros and cons: what Arizona homeowners should know

By Andy Johnson, Owner · Updated July 2026 · 7 minute read

Metal roofs are having a moment, and in Arizona a lot of the hype is actually justified: they last 40 to 70 years, reflect our brutal sun instead of soaking it up, and barely need maintenance. But they cost real money upfront and they're not right for every house or every street. Here's the honest weighing, from a roofer who installs metal, tile, and shingle and doesn't care which one you pick as long as it's the right call for your home.

Key takeaways

  • Metal roofs last 40 to 70 years, two to three times the life of asphalt shingles, and shrug off the heat and wind that wear other materials out.
  • Reflecting solar heat instead of absorbing it can cut cooling costs meaningfully, a bigger deal in Arizona than almost anywhere.
  • The trade-offs are real: higher upfront cost, potential noise without proper insulation, and denting from severe impacts.

Advantages of metal roofing

Durability and longevity

A metal roof routinely outlives two or three shingle roofs. It handles the two things Arizona throws hardest, relentless UV and monsoon wind, without cracking, curling, or losing granules, and steel panels resist hail and debris better than most alternatives. Standing seam designs add concealed fasteners and raised seams that improve water shedding and reduce leak points. In a climate where sun damage is the number one roof killer, that durability is worth a lot.

Energy efficiency

This is metal's best Arizona argument. Instead of absorbing solar heat the way dark shingles do, metal reflects it, and reflective coatings amplify the effect. Industry figures put cooling savings up to around 25 percent, and in a place where cooling dominates the summer power bill, the roof pays some of its own way every year. Metal consistently ranks among the top energy-efficient roof types for hot climates.

Low maintenance

Metal doesn't crack, rot, or shed granules. Annual upkeep is essentially an inspection and keeping debris out of valleys. Compared to the recoating schedule of a foam roof or the underlayment renewals of tile, metal asks the least of you over its life.

Disadvantages of metal roofing

Higher upfront cost

Metal costs roughly twice what asphalt shingles do upfront, sometimes more depending on the panel style. The long-run math often favors metal because you're not buying a second and third roof, but the first check is bigger, and that's a genuine barrier. Where the budget is the deciding factor, my cost guide and financing options lay out the real numbers.

Noise during rain and hail

The old barn-roof drumming is mostly a solved problem, solid decking and proper underlayment quiet a modern metal roof to near-shingle levels, but "mostly solved" depends on the installer doing it right. Ask specifically how the assembly handles sound before you sign.

Potential for denting

Severe hail or a falling branch can dent metal panels. Steel resists this better than aluminum or copper. Dents are usually cosmetic rather than functional, but on a visible slope, cosmetic matters.

Types of metal roofing materials

  • Steel: the workhorse. Strong, dent-resistant, coated (galvanized or Galvalume) against corrosion. Best value for most Arizona homes.
  • Aluminum: lighter and naturally corrosion-proof, but softer, so more prone to denting. Its corrosion advantage matters more on the coast than in the desert.
  • Copper and zinc: premium metals that can last a century and develop a distinctive patina. Beautiful, expensive, and rare on East Valley homes.

Design options

  • Standing seam: vertical panels with raised, concealed-fastener seams. The modern look, and the best water performance.
  • Metal shingles: panels stamped to mimic shingle, shake, or tile, for homeowners who want metal's life without the industrial look.
  • Color choices: broad, from charcoal and matte black to lighter reflective tones. In Arizona, lighter colors earn their keep on the cooling bill.

Property value and safety

Metal roofs are strong resale features: national remodeling data consistently shows metal recouping a large share of its installation cost at sale, and buyers understand "this roof outlasts the mortgage." On safety, metal carries a Class A fire rating, resists impact, and properly installed systems are engineered for winds well beyond what our monsoons deliver. A quality roof of any material also helps at resale time.

Environmental benefits

Metal roofing typically contains significant recycled content and is fully recyclable at end of life, unlike shingles, which go to landfill by the ton. Add the decades-longer replacement cycle and the cooling energy saved, and metal is the strongest sustainability story in residential roofing.

Common misconceptions

  • "Metal roofs are loud." Not with modern decking and underlayment. Ask how the assembly is built.
  • "Metal attracts lightning." It doesn't. Metal is actually safer in a strike because it disperses energy and doesn't ignite.
  • "Metal rusts." Modern coatings handle corrosion for decades, especially in our dry climate.

Common questions

How long does a metal roof last compared to shingles?

40 to 70 years versus roughly 15 to 25 for asphalt shingles in Arizona heat. One metal roof spans two or three shingle cycles.

Are metal roofs energy efficient in Arizona?

Yes, genuinely. Reflecting solar heat instead of absorbing it can trim cooling costs by up to a quarter, and Arizona's cooling-heavy bills make that worth more here than in mild climates.

Is metal or tile better for Arizona?

Both handle our climate well. Tile fits the regional look and costs less; metal lasts longer with less maintenance. It usually comes down to budget, aesthetics, and how long you'll own the home. My best roofing types for Arizona guide compares the full field.
