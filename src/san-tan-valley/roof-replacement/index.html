Is it time?

Why first roofs come due early out here

Here's the part nobody mentioned when you signed at the builder's office: on a tile roof, the tile is armor, and the felt underlayment beneath it is the actual waterproofing. The crews that built out Johnson Ranch, San Tan Heights and Copper Basin in the 2000s were moving at full speed, and the felt they were laying down was whatever kept the schedule. Arizona heat cooks entry-grade felt on a timetable the tiles never keep.

So the roof looks perfect from the street while the waterproofing under it quietly runs out. When that happens, the honest fix is usually a tile underlayment replacement: we lift your tiles, re-felt, and relay the same tiles. You get decades more roof without paying for tile you already own. Your builder picked your first underlayment. The second one is your call.

Watch for the warning signs anyway: cracked or missing tiles, water stains on ceilings or walls, a sagging roofline, musty smells in the attic, or leaks after monsoon rain. If you're seeing any of those, start with a free inspection and I'll tell you whether it's a repair, a replacement, or nothing at all yet.

How long does a roof replacement take in San Tan Valley?

Most homes take 4 to 5 days depending on the size and complexity of the roof. My crew protects your landscaping and driveway and cleans up at the end of every day, not just at the end of the job.

Can you reuse my existing roof tiles?

Usually, yes. San Tan Valley's concrete tile is still young and healthy on almost every roof I walk out here. We lift the tiles, replace the failed underlayment beneath, and relay the same tiles. You pay for new waterproofing, not for tile you already own.

I'm the first owner and the house isn't that old. Why would it need a new roof?

Because the builder picked the underlayment, not you. The crews that built out Johnson Ranch, San Tan Heights and Copper Basin in the 2000s mostly used entry-grade felt, and Arizona heat wears that out years before the tiles. The tiles are fine. The waterproofing under them is what comes due, and replacing just that costs far less than a full new roof.
